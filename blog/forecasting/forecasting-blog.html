Teaching Language Models the Language of Time: A Journey into Financial Forecasting

December 17, 2024 18 min read

How foundation models pre-trained on time series data challenge traditional forecasting methods, achieving remarkable zero-shot performance on financial data while revealing surprising limitations in uncertainty quantification.

MSc AI, University of Amsterdam

What if I told you that a model trained to predict the next word in a sentence could also predict tomorrow's bank account balance? This seemingly absurd proposition lies at the heart of a revolution in time series forecasting—one that challenges everything we thought we knew about predicting the future from data.

The Forecasting Revolution

In 2024, millions evacuated their homes in Florida based on AI weather predictions. The model responsible? GraphCast, a foundation model that predicted Hurricane Milton's landfall 18 hours earlier than traditional methods. This is the promise of foundation models—massive neural networks that learn universal patterns from vast datasets and apply them to new, unseen problems.

My research at ING Bank explored whether this same revolution could transform financial forecasting. Could a model trained on diverse time series—from electricity consumption to heartbeat patterns—accurately predict bank account balances it had never seen before?

From Words to Numbers: The Chronos Framework

The key innovation lies in treating time series like language. Just as ChatGPT predicts the next word in a sentence, Chronos predicts the next value in a sequence. But there's a catch: language has a finite vocabulary, while numerical values are infinite. How do we bridge this gap?

The magic happens through quantization: continuous values are mapped to a finite set of bins, creating a "vocabulary" of numerical tokens. This allows the T5 language model architecture to process time series just like text.

The Experiment: 278 Financial Time Series

Working with ING's transaction data, I analyzed 278 aggregated account balance time series spanning nearly three years. Each series represented complex financial behaviors— from steady corporate accounts to volatile trading portfolios. The challenge: could Chronos, without any training on this specific data, outperform models explicitly designed and trained for this task?

The Confidence Crisis

But here's where things get interesting. While Chronos excelled at point predictions, its confidence intervals told a different story. The model was consistently overconfident, producing narrow uncertainty bands that failed to capture the true variability of financial data.

Understanding the Patterns

Financial time series aren't created equal. Some follow predictable seasonal patterns— think monthly salary deposits or quarterly tax payments. Others exhibit high entropy, bouncing unpredictably with market movements. How do different models handle this diversity?

Time Series Characteristics vs. Model Performance

Models perform best on series with clear seasonal patterns. Foundation models show particular strength in detecting hidden periodicities that traditional methods miss.

Lessons from the Frontier

This research reveals both the promise and pitfalls of applying foundation models to financial forecasting. The key insights:

1

Zero-Shot Superiority

Foundation models can match or exceed task-specific models without any training on financial data, suggesting they learn universal temporal patterns.

2

The Uncertainty Gap

While accurate in point predictions, foundation models struggle with uncertainty quantification—critical for risk management applications.

3

Fine-Tuning Paradox

Surprisingly, fine-tuning Chronos on financial data didn't improve performance—and actually worsened confidence calibration.

4

Context Matters

Performance varies dramatically based on time series characteristics. Simple patterns favor traditional methods; complex patterns favor foundation models.

The Future of Financial Forecasting

As foundation models continue to evolve, their role in financial forecasting will likely expand. The ability to leverage patterns learned from diverse domains—applying lessons from weather patterns to stock movements, or from heartbeats to transaction flows—opens new possibilities for understanding financial dynamics.

Yet challenges remain. The overconfidence problem isn't just a technical issue—it's a fundamental question about how these models understand uncertainty. Future research must address this gap before foundation models can be trusted with critical financial decisions.

Practical Implications

For practitioners in financial institutions, these findings suggest a nuanced approach to adopting foundation models:

  • For point forecasting: Foundation models offer immediate value, especially for diverse portfolios where training individual models is impractical.
  • For risk assessment: Traditional methods or calibrated deep learning models remain more reliable due to better uncertainty quantification.
  • For rapid prototyping: Zero-shot capabilities enable quick experimentation without extensive data preparation or training.
  • For production systems: Consider ensemble approaches that combine foundation model accuracy with traditional model reliability.

A Practical Ensemble Approach

# Combine foundation model accuracy with traditional uncertainty
class EnsembleForecaster:
    def __init__(self):
        self.chronos = ChronosPipeline.from_pretrained("amazon/chronos-t5-large")
        self.arima = AutoARIMA(season_length=[7, 30])
        
    def forecast(self, time_series, horizon=30):
        # Get point predictions from Chronos
        chronos_pred = self.chronos.predict(
            time_series, 
            prediction_length=horizon
        ).median(axis=0)
        
        # Get confidence intervals from ARIMA
        arima_fit = self.arima.fit(time_series)
        arima_intervals = arima_fit.predict_interval(horizon)
        
        # Combine: Chronos accuracy + ARIMA uncertainty
        return {
            'prediction': chronos_pred,
            'lower_bound': chronos_pred - (arima_intervals.width / 2),
            'upper_bound': chronos_pred + (arima_intervals.width / 2),
            'confidence': 0.9
        }

# Usage
forecaster = EnsembleForecaster()
results = forecaster.forecast(account_balances, horizon=30)
print(f"30-day forecast: {results['prediction'][-1]:.2f}")
print(f"90% CI: [{results['lower_bound'][-1]:.2f}, {results['upper_bound'][-1]:.2f}]")

Conclusion: The Dawn of a New Era

The success of foundation models in financial forecasting marks a paradigm shift. We're moving from an era of specialized, task-specific models to one where general-purpose AI systems can tackle diverse prediction challenges out of the box.

Yet this shift brings new responsibilities. As these models become more powerful, understanding their limitations becomes crucial. The overconfidence problem I discovered isn't just a technical curiosity—it's a warning that even the most advanced AI systems have blind spots.

The future of financial forecasting lies not in choosing between traditional methods and foundation models, but in understanding how to combine their complementary strengths. As we stand at this crossroads, one thing is clear: the language of time has found a new translator, and finance will never be the same.
